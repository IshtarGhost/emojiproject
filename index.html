Stanley Kubrick Tribute Site

Stanley Kubrick was born in New York, NY to Sadie Gertrude (Perveler) and Dr. Jacob Kubrick, MD. Two key childhood events shaped Stanley's future. First, his father taught him to play chess. His passion for the game vastly improved his ability to focus. Second, Stanley was given a camera for his 13th birthday, and he excelled at photography. He managed to sell an unsolicited photograph to Look Magazine, which turned into a job as the magazine's Staff Photographer. As a film Director, Stanley created shots as detailed as magazine photographs.

Stanley and a friend made a documentary called Day of the Fight (1951), which led to shooting the documentaries Flying Padre (1951) and The Seafarers (1953).

With investor backing, Kubrick made Fear and Desire (1952). However, Kubrick's marriage didn't survive the shoot. Despite mixed reviews, Kubrick demonstrated his talent as a Director, and Hollywood took notice. Next, he directed Killer's Kiss (1955) and The Killing (1956). In 1957, he directed Kirk Douglas in Paths of Glory (1957). Douglas later called upon Kubrick to take over the production of Spartacus (1960).

Kubrick's first UK film was an adaptation of Nabokov's controversial Lolita (1962). Next, Kubrick released Dr. Strangelove (1964), which was originally written as a drama. Kubrick brilliantly turned it into a satire.

Kubrick again adapted a novel to make 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968), which is still listed as an AFI Top 20 Film. Kubrick followed with A Clockwork Orange (1971), which caused a huge controversy because its violent content.

Barry Lyndon (1975) was life changing for Kubrick. While filming in Ireland, he was warned about being targeted by the IRA. After, Kubrick became a recluse.

Kubrick declined an offer to direct a sequel to The Exorcist, to make his own horror film, The Shining (1980). Next, he released Full Metal Jacket (1987). By this time, Kubrick was re-married with children. Kubrick's last film was Eyes Wide Shut (1999), which he considered his best film.
